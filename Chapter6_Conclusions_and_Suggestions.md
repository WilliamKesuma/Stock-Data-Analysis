## 6.1. Conclusions

This study was conducted to address the research problem formulated in Sub-chapter 1.2: after developing the ARIMA and SARIMA forecasting models, which model demonstrates higher accuracy and better performance in predicting stock price movements within the NASDAQ-100 index? Based on the complete implementation, testing, and discussion presented in Chapters 4 and 5, the following conclusions are drawn:

1. Both the ARIMA and SARIMA models demonstrated highly accurate forecasting performance for daily stock price prediction across all five selected NASDAQ-100 stocks (AAPL, NVDA, MSFT, GOOGL, and AMZN). All ten model-stock combinations achieved MAPE values below 2%, which falls well within the "Highly Accurate Forecasting" category (MAPE < 10%) as defined by the Lewis (1982) classification. The best individual result was achieved by MSFT-SARIMA with a MAPE of 0.9527%, while the highest MAPE was 1.8681% (NVDA-SARIMA).

2. In direct comparison, the ARIMA model is identified as the more suitable model for daily stock price forecasting in the NASDAQ-100 index. ARIMA performed better or equal on three out of five stocks (AAPL, NVDA, and AMZN), while SARIMA outperformed on two stocks (MSFT and GOOGL). However, the performance differences between the two models were marginal across all stocks, with the largest MAPE difference being only 0.029 percentage points (MSFT). For AAPL and AMZN, both models produced identical results because the auto_arima process assigned a seasonal component of (0,0,0), making SARIMA functionally equivalent to ARIMA.

3. The seasonal extension provided by SARIMA did not yield meaningful improvements over ARIMA for daily stock price data. Four out of five stocks (AAPL, MSFT, GOOGL, and AMZN) received trivial seasonal components of (0,0,0), indicating that auto_arima detected no statistically significant weekly seasonal patterns. Only NVDA exhibited a non-trivial seasonal moving average component (Q = 1), yet this did not translate into improved forecasting accuracy. This finding suggests that daily closing prices of NASDAQ-100 stocks do not exhibit strong weekly seasonality, which is consistent with the efficient market hypothesis.

4. The forecasting results produced by both models can serve as objective, data-driven decision support information for investment decision-making, as stated in the research objective (Sub-chapter 1.3). With average forecast errors (MAE) ranging from $2.73 to $4.55 on stocks priced between approximately $150 and $500, the models provide reliable one-step-ahead price estimates that may assist investors in anticipating next-day price movements. However, as noted in the scope of study (Sub-chapter 1.5), these models rely solely on historical price patterns and do not incorporate external factors such as political conditions, government policies, macroeconomic news, or qualitative market sentiment. Therefore, the forecasting results should be used as one component of a broader investment analysis framework, not as the sole basis for trading decisions.

## 6.2. Suggestions

Based on the findings and limitations identified throughout this study, the following recommendations are proposed to support the improvement and further utilization of the forecasting analysis:

1. **Incorporate additional forecasting methods for comparison.** Future research could extend the comparative analysis by including more advanced models such as GARCH (Generalized Autoregressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity) for volatility modeling, or machine learning approaches such as LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory) neural networks. Comparing these methods against ARIMA and SARIMA would provide a more comprehensive understanding of which modeling approach is most effective for stock price forecasting, particularly for capturing non-linear patterns that traditional time series models may miss.

2. **Expand the scope of stocks and market indices.** This study focused on five technology-sector stocks within the NASDAQ-100 index. To improve the generalizability of the findings, future studies could include stocks from other sectors (e.g., healthcare, finance, energy) or from other major indices (e.g., S&P 500, Dow Jones Industrial Average). This would help determine whether the observed absence of weekly seasonality and the marginal ARIMA-SARIMA performance gap hold across different market segments.

3. **Integrate external variables into the forecasting framework.** As highlighted in the scope of study (Sub-chapter 1.5), this research focused exclusively on quantitative analysis of historical price data. Future work could incorporate exogenous variables — such as macroeconomic indicators, trading volume, market sentiment indices, or news-based features — using models like ARIMAX or SARIMAX. This integration may improve forecasting accuracy by capturing the influence of external factors on stock price movements, thereby enhancing the practical value of the models as decision support tools for investors.

4. **Explore multi-step-ahead forecasting horizons.** The current study employed a rolling one-step-ahead forecasting approach, which predicts only the next trading day. For practical investment applications, it would be valuable to evaluate the models' performance over longer forecasting horizons (e.g., 5-day, 10-day, or 21-day ahead forecasts). This would provide insights into how quickly forecast accuracy degrades over time and help investors determine the appropriate time horizon for using these models in their decision-making process.

5. **Develop an automated forecasting tool or dashboard.** To enhance the practical benefit outlined in Sub-chapter 1.4.2, the forecasting pipeline developed in this study could be packaged into an interactive application or web-based dashboard. Such a tool would allow investors to input a stock ticker and automatically receive updated forecasts with performance metrics, making the research output more accessible and directly applicable to real-world investment decision-making.
